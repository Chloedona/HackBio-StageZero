Can AI Discover New Genes We Didn’t Know Existed?

Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming the way we understand biology, particularly in the field of genomics. For many years, scientists believed that most genes within the human genome had already been identified. However, as sequencing technologies have improved, it has become clear that the genome is far more complex than first thought. Hidden within vast amounts of DNA data may be genes and functional elements we have not yet recognised.

Machine learning, a branch of AI that enables computers to identify patterns and make predictions from large datasets, is now helping researchers explore these unknown areas. Rather than relying solely on traditional laboratory methods, scientists can use AI to detect subtle signals in genomic data that may point to entirely new genes (Libbrecht and Noble, 2015).

Main Body

The human genome contains around three billion base pairs of DNA, yet only a small proportion directly codes for proteins (International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium, 2004). Historically, gene discovery focused mainly on these protein-coding regions because they were easier to identify and study. However, it is now understood that non-coding regions—once often dismissed as “junk DNA”—can play major roles in regulating gene activity, development, and disease (ENCODE Project Consortium, 2012).

This is where AI becomes particularly valuable. Machine learning models can analyse enormous genomic datasets far faster than humans and detect patterns that would otherwise be missed. For example, algorithms can compare DNA sequences across species, identify unusual patterns of gene expression, and predict whether a stretch of DNA may have a biological function (Eraslan et al., 2019).

AI is also supporting the discovery of non-coding RNAs, small RNA molecules that do not produce proteins but are essential for controlling how genes are switched on and off. Many of these were difficult to detect using conventional methods alone, but computational analysis has revealed consistent patterns in sequencing data, expanding our understanding of diseases such as cancer and genetic disorders (Esteller, 2011).

Deep learning, a more advanced form of machine learning, has pushed this even further. Models used in genome annotation can predict promoter regions, splice sites, and previously unrecognised transcripts. In simple terms, they help scientists map where genes begin, end, and how they are regulated. This improves both diagnosis and treatment strategies in personalised medicine (Min, Lee and Yoon, 2017). More recently, deep learning systems such as AlphaFold have demonstrated how AI can predict complex biological structures with remarkable accuracy, showing the wider potential of AI-driven discovery in molecular biology (Jumper et al., 2021).

Despite its advantages, AI also has important limitations. Machine learning models are only as reliable as the data they are trained on. Poor-quality or biased datasets can lead to incorrect predictions, while false positives may waste valuable laboratory time and resources. Predictions made by AI still require experimental validation to confirm whether a suspected genomic element truly functions as a gene. In addition, the growing use of genomic data raises ethical concerns around privacy, consent, and data ownership, particularly in personalised medicine.

AI should therefore be viewed as a powerful tool that supports scientists rather than replacing them. Human expertise remains essential for interpreting results and ensuring discoveries are biologically meaningful.

Conclusion

AI has opened a new chapter in genomics by allowing scientists to look beyond what is already known. Machine learning can uncover hidden genes, reveal important non-coding elements, and challenge older ideas about which parts of DNA matter most. As genomic datasets continue to grow, AI will become even more important in helping researchers make sense of this complexity.

Ultimately, AI is not replacing scientists but enhancing their ability to ask better questions and make faster discoveries. As computational biology continues to advance, AI may help reveal parts of the genome we have overlooked for decades, reshaping our understanding of life itself. Rather than asking whether AI can discover new genes, the real question may be how many important discoveries are still waiting to be found.
