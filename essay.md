# Can AI Discover New Genes We Didn’t Know Existed?

## Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) is changing the way we study biology, especially in genomics—the science of understanding DNA and genes. For many years, scientists believed that most human genes had already been discovered. However, as DNA sequencing technology has improved, researchers have realised that the genome is far more complex than first thought. Hidden within large amounts of DNA data may be genes and important functional regions that we still do not fully understand.

Machine learning, a type of AI that allows computers to recognise patterns and make predictions from large datasets, is helping scientists explore these unknown areas. Instead of relying only on traditional laboratory methods, researchers can now use AI to detect subtle patterns in DNA that may suggest the presence of entirely new genes (Libbrecht and Noble, 2015).

## Main Body

The human genome contains around three billion base pairs of DNA, but only a small percentage directly codes for proteins (International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium, 2004). Proteins are important molecules that carry out most functions inside cells. For many years, gene discovery focused mainly on these protein-coding regions because they were easier to identify and study.

However, scientists now know that non-coding regions—once described as “junk DNA”—can also play major roles in controlling gene activity, development, and disease (ENCODE Project Consortium, 2012). These hidden regions are much harder to study, which is where AI becomes especially useful.

One major benefit of AI in genomics is speed and pattern recognition. Machine learning models can analyse huge amounts of genomic data much faster than humans. They can compare DNA sequences across species, identify unusual gene activity, and predict whether a section of DNA may have an important biological function (Eraslan et al., 2019). This helps researchers discover genes that may have been missed using older methods. For example, AI has helped identify non-coding RNAs, which are small RNA molecules that do not make proteins but help control how genes are switched on and off (Esteller, 2011).

Another advantage is improved personalised medicine. More advanced AI systems, such as deep learning, can help predict how genes are regulated and how proteins fold into their final shape. Tools like AlphaFold have shown how AI can predict protein structures with remarkable accuracy, helping scientists better understand diseases and develop targeted treatments (Jumper et al., 2021).

However, there are also important limitations. One challenge is that AI models are only as good as the data they are trained on. If the data is poor-quality, incomplete, or biased, the predictions may be wrong. This can lead to false positives, where scientists spend time investigating genes that do not actually exist.

Another limitation is that AI cannot replace laboratory testing. A computer may predict that a section of DNA behaves like a gene, but experiments are still needed to prove whether it truly functions as one. There are also ethical concerns around storing and using large amounts of genomic data, especially regarding privacy and patient consent.

This shows that while AI is a powerful tool, human expertise is still essential. Scientists are needed to interpret results, test predictions, and make sure discoveries are biologically meaningful.

## Conclusion

AI is opening a new chapter in genomics by helping scientists look beyond what is already known. Machine learning can uncover hidden genes, identify important non-coding elements, and improve our understanding of how life works at a molecular level.

The biggest benefit of AI is its ability to process huge amounts of data quickly and recognise patterns that humans may miss. However, limitations such as poor-quality data, false predictions, and the need for laboratory validation show that AI cannot work alone.

Ultimately, AI is not replacing scientists—it is helping them make faster discoveries and ask better scientific questions. As technology continues to improve, AI may reveal parts of the genome that have been overlooked for decades, reshaping the future of biology and medicine.
