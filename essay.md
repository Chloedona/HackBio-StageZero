Can AI Discover New Genes We Didn’t Know Existed?
Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) is changing the way we study biology, especially in genomics—the science of understanding DNA and genes. For many years, scientists believed that most human genes had already been discovered. However, as DNA sequencing technology has improved, researchers have realised that the genome is far more complex than first thought. Hidden within large amounts of DNA data may be genes and important functional regions that we still do not fully understand.

Machine learning, a type of AI that allows computers to recognise patterns and make predictions from large datasets, is helping scientists explore these unknown areas. Instead of relying only on traditional laboratory methods, researchers can now use AI to detect subtle patterns in DNA that may suggest the presence of entirely new genes (Libbrecht and Noble, 2015).

Main Body

The human genome contains around three billion base pairs of DNA, but only a small percentage directly codes for proteins (International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium, 2004). Proteins are important molecules that carry out most functions inside cells. For many years, gene discovery focused mainly on these protein-coding regions because they were easier to identify and study.

However, scientists now know that non-coding regions—once described as “junk DNA”—can also play major roles in controlling gene activity, development, and disease (ENCODE Project Consortium, 2012). These hidden regions are much harder to study, which is where AI becomes especially useful.

One major benefit of AI is speed and pattern recognition. Machine learning models can analyse huge amounts of genomic data much faster than humans. They can compare DNA sequences across species, identify unusual gene activity, and predict whether a section of DNA may have an important biological function (Eraslan et al., 2019). This helps researchers discover genes that may have been missed using older methods.

AI has also helped scientists discover non-coding RNAs. These are small RNA molecules that do not make proteins but help control how genes are switched on and off. Many of these were difficult to detect using older methods, but AI-based analysis has revealed their role in diseases such as cancer (Esteller, 2011).

More advanced systems, such as deep learning, can predict where genes start and end, how they are regulated, and even how proteins fold into their final shape. For example, AlphaFold has shown how AI can predict protein structures with remarkable accuracy, demonstrating the wider potential of AI in biological research (Jumper et al., 2021).

However, AI also has important limitations. Machine learning models are only as good as the data they are trained on. If the data is poor-quality, incomplete, or biased, the predictions may be wrong. False positives can waste valuable laboratory time if scientists investigate genes that do not actually exist.

AI predictions must also be tested in the lab. A computer may suggest that a DNA sequence behaves like a gene, but experiments are still needed to prove whether it truly functions as one. There are also ethical concerns around the use of genomic data, especially regarding privacy and consent in personalised medicine.

For these reasons, AI should be seen as a powerful support tool rather than a replacement for scientists.

Conclusion

AI is opening a new chapter in genomics by helping scientists look beyond what is already known. Machine learning can uncover hidden genes, identify important non-coding elements, and improve our understanding of how life works at a molecular level.

One of its greatest benefits is the ability to process huge amounts of data quickly and identify patterns that humans may miss. However, challenges such as inaccurate predictions, poor-quality data, and ethical concerns show that AI cannot work alone.

Ultimately, AI is not replacing scientists—it is helping them ask better questions and make faster discoveries. As technology continues to improve, AI may reveal parts of the genome that have been overlooked for decades, reshaping the future of biology and medicine.
